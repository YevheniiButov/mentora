üü¶ Card 15: Principles of pulp therapy in pediatric dentistryThe goal of pulp therapy in primary and young permanent teeth is to maintain the integrity and health of the teeth and supporting tissues. Preservation of the pulp vitality whenever possible is emphasized to allow for continued root development in young permanent teeth (apexogenesis) or to maintain primary teeth until natural exfoliation. When vitality cannot be maintained, removal of infected or necrotic pulp tissue and proper restoration are necessary. ### üìò –†–∞–∑–¥–µ–ª 5: Pediatric Dental Trauma

**üü¶ Card 23: Classification of dental trauma in children**  
Dental trauma in children includes injuries to both primary and permanent teeth and is classified according to the type of tissue affected: enamel fractures, enamel-dentin fractures, complicated crown fractures (with pulp exposure), root fractures, luxation injuries (concussion, subluxation, lateral luxation, extrusive luxation, intrusive luxation), and avulsion. The classification system most often used is based on Andreasen's trauma guidelines. Understanding the type of trauma is critical for planning the appropriate treatment and prognosis.  
**–¢–µ–≥–∏:** Pedodontics, Endodontics-Diagnosis-Pulp, Oral-Surgery-Complications  
**source_references:** ["Pediatric Dentistry, Chapter 29"]

**üü¶ Card 24: Management of luxation injuries in primary teeth**  
In luxation injuries involving primary teeth, management aims to avoid damage to developing permanent teeth. Minor injuries such as concussion and subluxation typically require observation only. In lateral, extrusive, or intrusive luxations, decisions depend on the severity of displacement. Severely displaced teeth are often extracted to prevent potential harm to the permanent successor. Intruded primary teeth may re-erupt spontaneously if displaced toward the labial bone plate; otherwise, extraction may be indicated. Close follow-up is mandatory to monitor healing and detect complications like ankylosis or infection.  
**–¢–µ–≥–∏:** Pedodontics, Oral-Surgery-Complications, Endodontics-Treatment  
**source_references:** ["Pediatric Dentistry, Chapter 29"]

üü¶ Card 25: Management of crown and root fractures in childrenCrown fractures without pulp involvement are treated with simple composite restoration or fragment reattachment. In complicated crown fractures involving the pulp, partial pulpotomy (Cvek pulpotomy) is often the treatment of choice in immature permanent teeth to preserve vitality. Root fractures require repositioning and splinting of the coronal fragment for 4 weeks to several months depending on the location of the fracture. Regular vitality testing and radiographic follow-up are essential. Healing occurs via hard tissue, connective tissue, or with pulp necrosis.–¢–µ–≥–∏: Pedodontics, Endodontics-Treatment, Oral-Surgery-Complicationssource_references: ["Pediatric Dentistry, Chapter 30"]

Antibiotic therapy and tetanus prophylaxis are considered. Endodontic treatment is initiated 7‚Äì10 days after replantation unless the tooth is immature with an attempt to allow revascularization. Long-term follow-up for detection of root resorption is critical.–¢–µ–≥–∏: Pedodontics, Endodontics-Treatment, Medical-Emergenciessource_references: ["Pediatric Dentistry, Chapter 31"]

üü¶ Card 27: Differences in trauma management: primary vs permanent teethTrauma management differs significantly between primary and permanent teeth. In primary teeth, the priority is to minimize risk to the developing permanent tooth germ, often opting for extraction rather than complex treatment. In permanent teeth, the goal is to preserve the tooth whenever possible to maintain esthetics and function. Intrusive luxations, for example, may be allowed to re-erupt in primary teeth but often require orthodontic or surgical repositioning in permanent teeth. Understanding developmental anatomy is crucial for correct treatment planning.–¢–µ–≥–∏: Pedodontics, Endodontics-Treatment, Oral-Surgery-Complicationssource_references: ["Pediatric Dentistry, Chapter 29"]

üü¶ Card 28: Complications after dental trauma in childrenPost-traumatic complications include pulp necrosis, pulp canal obliteration, root resorption (inflammatory or replacement), ankylosis, discoloration, and disturbances in eruption of permanent teeth. Timely diagnosis and intervention, such as decoronation in ankylosed teeth, may be needed to preserve alveolar bone. ### üìò –†–∞–∑–¥–µ–ª 6: Preventive Dentistry and Fluoride

**üü¶ Card 30: Principles of preventive dentistry in children**  
Preventive dentistry focuses on early identification of risk factors, patient and parent education, implementation of protective strategies, and reduction of future treatment needs. Emphasis is placed on anticipatory guidance, early dental visits (by age one), dietary counseling, oral hygiene instruction, fluoride use, and sealant application. Preventive strategies should be tailored individually based on caries risk assessment. An interdisciplinary approach involving pediatricians, nutritionists, and public health programs strengthens preventive outcomes.  
**–¢–µ–≥–∏:** Pedodontics, Preventive-Dentistry-Hygiene, Cariology

**üü¶ Card 31: Role of fluoride in caries prevention**  
Fluoride acts through multiple mechanisms: enhancing remineralization of enamel, inhibiting demineralization, and reducing cariogenic bacterial activity. Systemic fluoride (e.g., community water fluoridation) and topical fluoride (toothpastes, varnishes, gels) both contribute to caries prevention. Fluoride varnishes are particularly useful in young and high-risk children. Proper dosage and application technique are crucial to maximize benefits and minimize the risk of dental fluorosis. Patient-specific fluoride recommendations depend on caries risk level and fluoride exposure history.  
**–¢–µ–≥–∏:** Pedodontics, Cariology, Preventive-Dentistry-Hygiene  
**source_references:** ["Pediatric Dentistry, Chapter 34"]

üü¶ Card 32: Fluoride delivery methods and recommended schedulesFluoride can be delivered systemically (water fluoridation, supplements) and topically (toothpastes, varnishes, gels, mouth rinses). Community water fluoridation at 0.7 ppm remains the most cost-effective public health measure. Fluoride varnishes are applied professionally every 3‚Äì6 months for high-risk children. Daily use of fluoride toothpaste (1000‚Äì1500 ppm) starting with the eruption of the first tooth is strongly recommended. Prescription-strength fluoride supplements are considered for children without adequate community water fluoridation. Correct dosage is based on the child's age and local water fluoride levels.–¢–µ–≥–∏: Pedodontics, Preventive-Dentistry-Hygiene, Pharmacology-Clinicalsource_references: ["Pediatric Dentistry, Chapter 34"]

üü¶ Card 33: Indications for dental sealants in childrenSealants are indicated for the prevention of occlusal caries in permanent molars and premolars with deep pits and fissures. They are particularly recommended for high-caries-risk children and adolescents. First permanent molars should ideally be sealed as soon as fully erupted. Resin-based sealants are preferred due to superior retention, but glass ionomer sealants may be used when moisture control is difficult. Sealants are cost-effective and significantly reduce the incidence of caries when applied and maintained properly. Regular recall and evaluation of sealant integrity are necessary.–¢–µ–≥–∏: Pedodontics, Preventive-Dentistry-Hygiene, Restorative-Materials-Directsource_references: ["Pediatric Dentistry, Chapter 35"]

üü¶ Card 34: Community-based preventive programsPublic health initiatives such as school fluoride rinse programs, dental sealant programs, anticipatory guidance campaigns, and community water fluoridation are crucial components of preventive pediatric dentistry. These programs aim to reduce disparities in oral health outcomes, particularly among underserved populations. Collaboration between dental professionals, schools, public health agencies, and policymakers is necessary for effective program design and implementation. Education of parents and children remains the foundation of community prevention efforts. Monitoring and evaluation ensure continuous improvement of preventive strategies.–¢–µ–≥–∏: Pedodontics, Preventive-Dentistry-Hygiene, Public-Health-Dentistrysource_references: ["Pediatric Dentistry, Chapter 36"]

### üìò –†–∞–∑–¥–µ–ª 7: Behavior Management in Pediatric Dentistry

**üü¶ Card 35: Principles of behavior management in pediatric dentistry**

**üü¶ Card 48: Pain assessment in pediatric dental patients**  
Pain assessment in children relies on both self-reported measures (when developmentally possible) and observational tools. ### üìò –†–∞–∑–¥–µ–ª 11: Dental Infections and Pediatric Emergencies

**üü¶ Card 52: Common odontogenic infections in pediatric patients**  
Odontogenic infections in children typically originate from untreated dental caries progressing to pulp necrosis and periapical pathology. Common presentations include localized abscesses, cellulitis, and space infections (e.g., submandibular, buccal spaces). Symptoms include swelling, pain, fever, trismus, and malaise. Early identification and intervention are critical to prevent complications such as airway obstruction or systemic spread. Management involves drainage, antimicrobial therapy, and definitive treatment of the source (e.g., pulpectomy, extraction).

**üü¶ Card 53: Antibiotic use in pediatric dental infections**  
Antibiotic therapy is indicated in pediatric dental infections with systemic involvement (fever, lymphadenopathy), rapid progression, or inability to establish drainage. Overprescription must be avoided to minimize antibiotic resistance.
